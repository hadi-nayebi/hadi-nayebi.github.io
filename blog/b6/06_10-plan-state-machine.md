# The Plan File — Long-Horizon Memory

*Essay 6.10 — The Markov Phasic Brain, Part 10 of 10.*

---

[Essay 6.9](06_9-gmode.html) opened the off-cycle lane — the deliberate maintenance side-quest where the phase gates are dropped so the operator can do work that doesn't fit the standard OPEVC shape. Gmode covers a *short* horizon: hours, one sitting, one ceremony. This final essay covers the opposite horizon — work that doesn't fit inside a single cycle and shouldn't. Work that needs to remember itself across days and dozens of cycles.

The mechanism is small. One field on the job object, one document on disk, and a counting rule that decides when the work is done. The discipline it produces is what turns the markov brain from single-session cognition into something that can hold a plan in its head for weeks. *[ref: plan-file-is-long-horizon-substrate | CONTEXT.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | The canonical glossary names the plan file (`.md` or `.yaml`) as "the cross-cycle durable handoff vessel. It persists across activations and accumulates intelligence run-over-run." The lifecycle is governed by the job's `cycle` counter and the plan's declared cycle count — not by a separate state machine. The plan file is born in cycle 1 EXECUTE, never at job creation.]*

This essay is for you — especially when your work takes longer than one sitting and the plan needs to outlive any single cycle.

---

## What the plan file owns

The plan file owns the long-horizon memory of a job. One field on the job object inside `data.json` — `plan_file` — carries it: the literal `false` when the job has no plan, or the name of a working document on disk (`<name>.md` or `<name>.yaml`) when it does. The document is the memory; the field is the pointer. *[ref: plan-file-field-three-values | CONTEXT.md "Job Stage" section | The canonical glossary defines `plan_file` as a single value per job — one of `false` (no plan), `<name>.md`, or `<name>.yaml`. A once-only guard prevents flipping the extension on the same job. The job's Stage is read directly from this shape: `false` is Stage 1, `.md` is Stage 2, `.yaml` is Stage 3.]*

The plan file is born in cycle 1 EXECUTE — not at job creation. Every job is created the same minimal way: a name, a short objective, and `plan_file = false`. Nothing on disk yet. The decision of whether this job *needs* a long-horizon document — and what shape it takes — is made later, once the agent has actually looked at the work. *[ref: plan-file-born-cycle-1-execute | CONTEXT.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | The glossary is explicit: "The plan file is BORN in cycle 1 EXECUTE, not at job creation — the creating phase only sets `plan_file = false`." Pending jobs always carry `plan_file = false`; the cycle-1 PLAN of the activated job owns the decision of whether to set a plan file at all. EXECUTE is the phase that creates non-CLAUDE.md files, so it is the phase that writes the plan document to disk.]*

What lives where matters. Job-scoped facts live on the job object; file-scoped facts live in the file. Plan files carry a small identification frontmatter — a `job:` line and a `plan_file:` line declaring which job they belong to — plus a `total_cycles:` count (for `.md`) or a `cycles:` list (for `.yaml`) declaring how many cycles the work is planned to take. There is no lifecycle-state field; the job's progress is read directly from its `cycle` counter against that declared total. *[ref: plan-frontmatter-and-cycle-count | CONTEXT.md "Job Stage" section | Stage 2 plans carry "required YAML frontmatter declaring `total_cycles: <N>`"; Stage 3 plans declare a `cycles:` list whose `max(c.cycle for c in cycles)` is the total. The job's completion eligibility is computed from `current_cycle` against that total (see "Cycle counter behavior"), not tracked by a separate state field.]*

## The Stages

A job's *Stage* is its classification by plan-file shape, and it is decided in **cycle 1 PLAN** — not at creation. The cleanest mental model: cycle 1 of any multi-cycle job is for creating or updating its plan; cycle 2 onward is for the actual work. Fresh job? Cycle 1 creates the plan. Re-activated job whose plan already exists? Cycle 1 updates it. Real work always starts cycle 2. *[ref: stage-decided-cycle-1-plan | CONTEXT.md "Job Stage" section | The glossary: "Stage is decided in cycle 1 PLAN of the new job, NOT at creation. Every pending job is born `plan_file = false` per the Universal starter shape; the cycle 1 PLAN phase's `set-plan-file` call (or non-call) picks the Stage." The Stage is read from `plan_file`'s shape, and the decision is owned by the activated job's first PLAN phase — not by whatever created it.]*

**Stage 1 — single-cycle, collaborative.** Cycle 1 PLAN never calls `set-plan-file`; `plan_file` stays `false`. There is no document on disk. The entire OPEVC pass holds the work in working memory and commits the artifacts directly. Single fixes, single updates, a freestyle teaching conversation — anything that closes in one cycle. Backward edges happen *inside* the cycle, between phases, not across cycles. A Stage-1 job can also be the seed from which a repeatable Stage-2 version grows: at CONDENSE, the agent can emit a `[PENDING-JOB]` marker proposing the multi-cycle version of the same work. *[ref: stage-1-no-plan-file | CONTEXT.md "Job Stage" section | Stage 1 is defined as "single-cycle, collaborative. Cycle 1 PLAN does NOT call `set-plan-file`; `plan_file` stays `false`. ONE cycle that loops internally via backward transitions … ONE CONDENSE at the end. CONDENSE asks `[JOB-COMPLETE]`. Used for one-off teaching, exploration, simple fixes."]*

**Stage 2 — repeatable, with an `.md` plan.** Cycle 1 PLAN calls `set-plan-file <name>.md`; cycle 1 EXECUTE writes the document at `.claude/knowledge/plans/<name>.md` with `total_cycles: <N>` in its frontmatter. The document becomes the long-horizon memory of this job: cycles 2..N do operational work against it, VERIFY refines it when a cycle surfaces a better criterion, and every cycle's PLAN re-reads it at phase entry. This is what carries a long job's understanding across days, after chat history has long since rolled off. *[ref: stage-2-md-plan | CONTEXT.md "Job Stage" section | Stage 2: "Cycle 1 PLAN calls `set-plan-file <id> <name>.md`. Cycle 1 EXECUTE creates the plan file at `.claude/knowledge/plans/<name>.md` with required YAML frontmatter declaring `total_cycles: <N>`. … Cycles 2..N do operational work against the plan. … Reactivatable — the plan file persists on disk; subsequent activations get smarter plans."]*

**Stage 3 — repeatable, with a `.yaml` plan.** Cycle 1 PLAN calls `set-plan-file <name>.yaml`; cycle 1 EXECUTE writes a structured document whose `cycles:` list declares each cycle's expected work. In completion semantics it is **identical to Stage 2** — cycle-1 plan creation, cycle-2..N work, the same counting rule deciding when the job is done. The only difference is the file format, and what that format unlocks: the `.yaml` can inject itself into the agent's context at every phase entry. A Stage-3 job is usually what a mature Stage-2 job graduates into after it has been run many times. *[ref: stage-3-identical-except-format | CONTEXT.md "Job Stage" section | Stage 3 is defined as "Otherwise IDENTICAL to Stage 2 in completion semantics … The ONLY difference between Stage 2 and Stage 3 is plan-file format. Inspired typically by a mature Stage-2 job repeated many times — NOT a state-flip on the Stage-2 itself." This corrects the older model that framed the `.yaml` as a separate dependent job spawned from the `.md` job.]*

A future *Stage 4* — a job that ships its own plugin, adding new gates and new voices — is named in the architecture but has no code behind it yet. *[ref: stage-4-aspirational | CONTEXT.md "Job Stage" section | "A future Stage 4 is named in B8.2 as the 'plugin form of a job' — a job that ships its own per-job plugin adding new hard gates and new voices. No code referent yet (aspirational per Rule 38's customization-gradient Layer 5)."]*

## How a multi-cycle job knows it is done

There is no state machine walking a plan through named stages. There is a counting rule.

Each multi-cycle plan declares its total — `total_cycles` for an `.md`, the length of the `cycles:` list for a `.yaml`. The job carries a `cycle` counter. The job becomes *eligible* to complete when its current cycle reaches the **effective last cycle**: the declared total, plus any extension cycles the run had to add when the original plan came up short. *[ref: effective-last-cycle-formula | CONTEXT.md "Cycle counter behavior" section | The glossary gives the formula `effective_last_cycle = (plan_file != false ? plan_file.total_cycles : 1) + extension_cycles_added`. The `[JOB-COMPLETE]` eligibility check at CONDENSE fires for Stage 2/3 when `current_cycle >= effective_last_cycle`. The `extension_cycles_added` term lets a run exceed its planned length when work surfaces mid-run (see "Extension cycle").]*

For a Stage-1 job there is no plan file, so `plan_file == false` makes it eligible to complete at *every* CONDENSE — every cycle is potentially the last. For Stage 2 and Stage 3, eligibility waits until the counter reaches the planned end. The value of `plan_file` is doing double duty: it names the document, and its presence-or-absence is what tells the completion gate which rule to apply. *[ref: completion-eligibility-by-plan-file | CONTEXT.md "[JOB-COMPLETE] completion hooks" section | The pre-completion hook's eligibility gate: "`plan_file == false` (Stage 1, fires every CONDENSE) OR `current_cycle >= effective_last_cycle` (Stage 2/3)." A single field, `plan_file`, both points at the document and discriminates Stage 1 from Stage 2/3 at the gate.]*

When the final cycle's CONDENSE arrives and the job is eligible, the agent asks the operator one structured question — `[JOB-COMPLETE]` — presenting the evidence that the objective is met. (That ceremony lands inside CONDENSE, the cognitive organ from [Essay 6.7](06_7-condense.html).) Approval flips the job to `completed`. The `plan_file` is not nulled or archived; it stays exactly where it is. *[ref: completion-plan-file-persists | CONTEXT.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | On approval: "`job.sh approve` flips `user_approval = true` → post-completion hook calls `job.sh complete` → status flips to `completed`. `plan_file` STAYS at its value across the completion." The plan is neither nulled nor moved on completion — it persists exactly so it can be re-read on the next activation.]*

That persistence is the whole point of the learning loop below.

VERIFY guards each cycle along the way. If a cycle's `## Outstanding Items` are not empty, VERIFY blocks the forward-advance and the agent loops back to PLAN or EXECUTE to resolve them. And if VERIFY *edits* the plan file — refines a vague criterion, drops one that can't be tested — it cannot forward-advance either; it backward-advances to PLAN, which re-cognizes and routes to OBSERVE for more context, to EXECUTE to redraft the plan, or straight back to VERIFY to re-test the updated plan without re-executing. *[ref: plan-verify-backward-loop | CONTEXT.md "Plan-verify backward loop" section | "Triggered when VERIFY edits the plan file … The custom VERIFY gate detects the plan-file edit and BLOCKS forward-advance. The agent calls `verify-commit.sh --backward plan` … PLAN re-cognizes and forwards to one of three destinations" — backward to OBSERVE, forward to EXECUTE, or forward to VERIFY directly (the one-line `plan:verify` FORWARD_MAP entry). No state-tracking field; the loop is built from existing transition primitives plus that one new entry.]*

## How `.yaml` injection turns the brain long-horizon

The `.md` is for humans. The `.yaml` is for the brain.

Once a Stage-3 job is running — its `plan_file` ends in `.yaml` — the phasic system's entry hook reads the `.yaml` on every phase transition and writes the phase-specific field-map to a cache file. The shared voice helper reads that cache when it fires any voice during the new phase, and yaml field-map entries augment the relevant voices — turning a job's structured long-horizon context into a stream of injections that show up automatically wherever the agent is reading guidance. *[ref: phase-init-yaml-injection-on-transition | .claude/plugins/phasic_system/hooks/phase-init.sh write_yaml_cache + phase-transition branch | The write_yaml_cache helper resolves `$ROOT_DIR/.claude/.tmp/yaml-injection-cache.json` and writes the field-map for the focused job's current phase by calling `bash "$PHASE_SH" --hook read-yaml`. The phase-transition branch of phase-init.sh calls write_yaml_cache after every advance, back, or commit-transition event so the freshly entered phase's yaml content immediately augments downstream voices. Gmode enter clears the cache (side-quest isolation); gmode exit restores it. New jobs and focus events also refresh. Net result: phase entry is the injection moment; the .yaml plays back the job's long-horizon memory each time the agent crosses a phase boundary.]*

The augmentation works in three modes — append, replace, and prepend. A bare-string yaml entry **appends** its text to the rendered voice after a blank line — the back-compat default. A structured entry of the form `{mode: replace, text: "..."}` **replaces** the rendered voice text entirely with the yaml content (useful when the job's context-specific voice should override the universal one). A `{mode: prepend, text: "..."}` entry **prepends** the yaml content before the rendered voice (useful for stamping a job-specific banner above the standard guidance). All three modes apply the same variable substitution as the underlying voice — `{{job_id}}`, `{{focused_name}}`, and the rest substitute identically into the yaml text. *[ref: voice-helper-three-mode-augmentation | .claude/plugins/lib/voice-helper/voice-helper.sh yaml-cognition block | The helper inspects the cache entry's JSON type per voice id. Bare string → APPEND (back-compat); object `{mode, text}` → switch on `mode` between `append` / `replace` / `prepend`; unknown mode falls back to APPEND so unrecognized values cannot silently drop a voice. The same KV_ARGS used to interpolate the xml voice text are re-applied to the yaml text via a preserved-args pattern so substitution keys stay symmetric across the two surfaces.]*

The yaml key IS the voice id directly — no transform, no naming convention. That makes the **callable voice catalog** the contract surface for layer-4 customization: a yaml plan can only target voices that some hook or script actually fires. A new helper builds the catalog by scanning every plugin's `voice.xml` for `id="..."` definitions and every non-test `.sh` / `.py` for quoted references. A defined voice with no callsite is an **orphan** — useless, because it never injects into context, and an invisible trap for yaml authors who target it and watch nothing happen. The seed agent ships with a recurring cleanup job pattern that consumes the orphan list and either links or removes each one over time. *[ref: voice-catalog-and-orphan-discipline | .claude/plugins/lib/yaml-cognition/build-voice-catalog.py + CLAUDE.md | The catalog tool enumerates defined ids across all `voice.xml` files plus quoted references in non-test plugin source (recognizing direct `get_voice "id"`, the `block "id"` wrapper, case-dispatch branches, and array enumerations like `COACHING_IDS=("id" ...)`). Modes: `--audit` (full report), `--list-callable` / `--list-orphans` / `--list-missing` (one id per line). The lib's CLAUDE.md names the contract: a yaml plan's keys must satisfy the callable set; orphans are scheduled for cleanup via a recurring yaml job, not allowed to linger as silent traps.]*

The yaml plan loader enforces the contract at validation time. When PLAN validates a new or edited `.yaml`, the schema check is followed by a key-vs-catalog check: every key under the phases map must match a callable voice id, otherwise the plan is rejected with a `did you mean` suggestion drawn from the closest matches. This catches the most common yaml authoring error — typing a voice id that no longer exists, or never did — before the job ever runs the cycle. *[ref: yaml-key-validator-rejects-uncallable-ids | .claude/plugins/lib/yaml-cognition/validate-yaml-voice-ids.py wired in .claude/plugins/phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh validate-yaml-format | The validator loads the callable set from the catalog builder, walks `cycles[*].phases.<phase>.<key>` (both current and legacy schema shapes), and rejects keys outside the callable set with `difflib.get_close_matches` suggestions. plan.sh's `validate-yaml-format` handler calls the validator AFTER the schema check passes; rejection fires the `error-yaml-voice-ids-invalid` voice with the path + flattened detail. Exit 0 = all keys callable; exit 1 = one or more invalid; exit 2 = catalog build error.]*

Planned jobs also skip the manual forecast ceremony each phase normally requires. Because a Stage-2 or Stage-3 job already declares its work in the plan file, every phase's init stamps the new entry's multiplier with `3` (the RAPID end of the scale) instead of the `0` sentinel that would otherwise leave tools locked until the agent calls `set-multiplier`. The forecast lives in the plan; the agent shouldn't be asked to re-forecast at every phase entry. The sentinel voice that nags for a multiplier naturally goes silent for these jobs because it only fires when the multiplier is `0`. *[ref: planned-jobs-auto-multiplier-3 | CONTEXT.md "Default multiplier" section | The canonical rule: planned jobs (Stage 2 `.md` AND Stage 3 `.yaml`) default the phase-entry multiplier to `3` (the RAPID end of the scale); Stage-1 jobs keep the sentinel `0` and forecast each phase consciously. The glossary explicitly bans a yaml-only version of this rule — both planned forms are covered, because both already declare their work in the plan file. (Prototype state: `job_core/scripts/job.sh default-multiplier` currently returns `3` for `.yaml`/`.yml` plans only; extending it to `.md` is Phase-D-coupled per the Addition ledger.)]*

This is the long-horizon mechanism. Without it, every cycle would start cold — the agent would have to re-read the plan and re-derive what each phase should know. With it, the agent's context grows back the long-form structure of the job automatically, each cycle, at every phase boundary. The brain doesn't have to remember to remember. *[ref: phase-entry-is-injection-moment | .claude/plugins/phasic_system/hooks/phase-init.sh:139-147 | The phase-transition branch calls write_yaml_cache after every advance, back, and commit-transition event. The write resolves the focused job's current phase and calls `bash "$PHASE_SH" --hook read-yaml` to extract the field-map for that phase, writing it to `$ROOT_DIR/.claude/.tmp/yaml-injection-cache.json`. Subsequent voices read the cache automatically. Without this hook, the cache wouldn't refresh on phase boundaries and the `.yaml` would be dead documentation; the hook IS the injection discipline.]*

The `.md` and `.yaml` are not redundant — they are different surfaces. The `.md` is a prose document a human can scan to follow the arc of a long job. The `.yaml` is a parseable structure the brain can inject at exactly the right moment. They share a basename so the agent knows they belong together, but they answer different needs. *[ref: md-yaml-coexist-different-surfaces | .claude/plugins/phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh validate-yaml-format | The validate-yaml-format command checks the `.yaml` against the phase.sh read-yaml schema — phases as keys, each carrying structured fields the injection target understands. The validator's existence is the point: the `.yaml` is *for* a parser. The `.md` carries no such validator and is never parsed; it is for the partner reading the long-horizon arc.]*

## The learning loop

A plan file that persists across completions is a plan file that can learn.

When a repeatable job finishes, it does not vanish. Its plan file stays on disk, and the job can be re-activated later — manually, or automatically on a repeating interval when the seed agent compacts and scans for due jobs. On re-activation the cycle counter resets to zero and the run starts fresh, but the plan file survives, and so does one quieter record: the job's `extension_contexts` — the running log of every time a past run had to add a cycle because the declared plan came up short. *[ref: extension-contexts-history-mode | CONTEXT.md "extension_contexts field" section | "Opt-in history mode: append-only across runs (NOT reset on reactivation). The array grows monotonically over the job's lifetime, accumulating one entry per `--add-cycle '<text>'` invocation across all activations." The cycle counter, `user_approval`, and `extension_cycles_added` all reset on reactivation; `extension_contexts[]`, `plan_file`, and `plugin_lock_approval` persist.]*

Cycle 1 of the new run reads that record. The OBSERVE entry voice surfaces it directly: prior runs added these late extensions; consider whether your refined plan should absorb them upfront. PLAN and EXECUTE then update the plan file — adjust `total_cycles`, rewrite a cycle's entry — so the next run plans for what the last run only discovered halfway through. *[ref: cycle-1-observe-reads-extension-history | CONTEXT.md "Extension cycle" section | On reactivation, "Cycle 1 of the repeat run reads the history. OBSERVE entry voice fires: 'Prior runs added these late extensions … Consider whether your refined PLAN this cycle should ABSORB these into the plan file (.md `total_cycles` adjustment + per-cycle entries, OR .yaml `cycles[]` entries) so future runs don't need to re-add them.'" The extensions are the learning trail across reactivations.]*

Over many runs the job converges toward needing no extensions at all. The plan stops being a guess and becomes an accurate forecast of the work, because each run wrote its surprises back into it. The learning is structural, not memorized — a future run reads the better plan directly, the same way it would read any other plan. This is what makes a repeating job get *smarter*, not just *repeat*. *[ref: learning-over-time-structural | CONTEXT.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | "The cycle-level VERIFY gate + job-level CONDENSE gate + cross-run `extension_contexts[]` history form a closed feedback loop. The plan file (`.md` or `.yaml`) absorbs experience run-over-run; the job converges toward 'no extensions needed' as the plan increasingly captures real scope. … The learning is structural, not memorized — future runs read the refined plan file directly."]*

## What would break without it

Take the plan file out. Now every multi-cycle job has to hold its own plan in working memory or re-derive it each session. The agent can still attempt long-horizon work, but each cycle starts cold — re-reading whatever notes survive in `knowledge/`, rebuilding intuition the previous cycle already had. Long jobs drift. A late cycle of a migration contradicts an early one because it never read the earlier plan. *[ref: plan-file-is-cross-cycle-memory | CONTEXT.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | The plan file exists precisely as "the cross-cycle durable handoff vessel" so phases can coordinate across cycles without rebuilding the contract every time. Remove it and each cycle reconstructs the plan from heuristics — the failure mode of chat-history-based agents on multi-session work.]*

Take the `.yaml` injection out. Now the long-horizon context is a document the agent has to *remember* to re-read every cycle. Sometimes it does. Sometimes it doesn't. Injection at phase entry is the discipline that turns "should re-read the plan" into "the plan re-reads itself into the context, every phase, every cycle." *[ref: injection-is-the-discipline | .claude/plugins/phasic_system/hooks/phase-init.sh:55-65 | The write_yaml_cache helper reads `${YAML_INJECTION_CACHE:-$ROOT_DIR/.claude/.tmp/yaml-injection-cache.json}` and writes the focused job's current-phase field-map. The cache only exists because the `.yaml` exists; without a parseable companion the voice helper has nothing to append and the agent gets generic voices instead of context-loaded ones. The hook turns "should re-read the plan" into automatic re-injection at every phase boundary.]*

Take the persistence out — null the plan file when the job completes. Now the learning loop dies. Every re-activation starts from a blank plan; the run repeats the same mistakes the last run already paid for. The plan can never become a forecast because nothing carries forward to forecast from. *[ref: persistence-enables-learning | CONTEXT.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | Because `plan_file` and `extension_contexts[]` both persist across completion ("`plan_file` STAYS at its value across the completion"; `extension_contexts[]` "STAYS in history mode"), the next activation can read the refined plan and the prior runs' extensions. Nulling the plan on completion would sever this loop — which is exactly why the plan file persists instead.]*

Each layer closes one failure mode of long-horizon work that ordinary chat-history-based agents fail at.

## What you would customize

The plan file is the clearest example of the seed's fourth customization layer — the place where a single job tailors the agent's behavior without touching a line of code. *[ref: plan-file-is-layer-4 | CONTEXT.md "Default multiplier" section | The `.yaml` plan customizes soft controls per job: its keys augment existing voices at phase entry, and its presence flips the default multiplier to RAPID for planned jobs. This is the customization-gradient's Layer 4 (per-job customization of soft controls) — distinct from editing hard gates (code) or voices (xml). The plan file is data the job carries, not machinery it changes.]*

You would design the **`.yaml` schema**. What does each phase need at entry? OBSERVE may want a `recall_targets:` list; PLAN a `decisions_locked:` map; EXECUTE a `forbidden_files:` array; VERIFY an `acceptance_criteria:` list with pass/fail rows. The prototype ships a minimal injection — roughly one objective per phase. Your seed will discover what richer fields produce richer cognition for your work, and the `.yaml` is where that discovery lives. *[ref: yaml-schema-is-customizable | .claude/plugins/phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh validate-yaml-format | The validate-yaml-format handler checks the `.yaml` against the phase.sh read-yaml contract — phases as keys, structured fields under each. The schema is enforced by the validator, but the validator is yours to edit: add expected fields, change rules, define new injection paths. The minimal version the prototype ships is a starting point, not a ceiling; the brain's long-horizon cognition is exactly as rich as the schema you author.]*

You would tune the **Stage decision**. The current rule lives in cycle-1 PLAN's judgment: a big project likely to span days and revisions gets a plan file; a one-off does not. If your domain has a clear threshold — every matter past a certain size goes multi-cycle — you can codify that as a hint voice that steers the classification. *[ref: stage-decision-is-judgment-shaped | CONTEXT.md "Job Stage" section | The Stage decision is owned by cycle 1 PLAN as narrative judgment, not a hard gate. Codifying a threshold means adding a voice or script that fires on a measurable trigger (size, prior-job similarity, domain tag) and steers the classification. The prototype keeps it judgment-shaped because the trade-offs differ per seed.]*

You would set the **repeating interval**. A job that should re-run on a rhythm — a weekly review, a daily digest — carries an interval; when it elapses, the seed agent's self-compact scan re-activates the job automatically, and the learning loop above kicks in run-over-run. *[ref: repeating-interval-customization | CONTEXT.md "Repeating job" section | A repeating job sets `repeating_interval` (integer hours, default 0 = one-shot); completed instances auto-reactivate via the self-compact rhythm when `now - last_completed_at >= repeating_interval`. Promotion happens via the CONDENSE-only `[REPEAT-JOB]` ceremony. This is the mechanism that turns a Stage-2/3 job into a self-maintaining recurring one.]*

What you would NOT do is remove the plan file. Take it out and the brain reverts to single-session cognition. The whole markov phasic discipline — the cycle, the cognitive metabolism organ, the multiplier, gmode — is built on the assumption that some jobs deserve to be remembered across cycles. The plan file is what makes that remembering deterministic instead of accidental.

Lift the pattern out of the seed agent and into a law practice. A partner running a long matter carries the plan file the same way: the prose plan accumulates across weekly client-review cycles in `matter.md`; once the matter is large enough to span many sessions, it graduates to a `matter.yaml` whose structured fields — scope, fee structure, retention terms, conflict-check results — inject at every revision phase, so each follow-up cycle inherits the matter's structure automatically instead of re-reading the prose. Run the same kind of matter again next quarter, and last quarter's surprises are already written into the plan. *[ref: pattern-lifts-to-professional-long-horizon | CONTEXT.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | The plan-file lifecycle — cycle-1 creation, cross-cycle persistence, `.yaml` injection at phase entry, run-over-run learning via `extension_contexts[]` — is generic enough that any long-horizon professional artifact (an engagement letter, a research protocol, a treatment plan, a project charter) fits the same shape. The Stage decision, the schema, and the repeating interval are the per-domain customization surface; the lifecycle stays constant.]*

The plan file is friction, not a wall. An operator in [gmode](06_9-gmode.html) can edit it directly with any editor. The injection assumes the phasic system fires on every phase entry; the counting rule assumes the agent honors the `[JOB-COMPLETE]` ceremony at the right cycle. The architecture is the ceremony; the discipline rests on the agent honoring it, not on the gate being unbreakable. *[ref: ceremony-not-tamperproof-barrier | CONTEXT.md "Phase" section | The glossary defines gmode as the "operator's privileged maintenance lane … unlocks `[PLUGIN-LOCK]` for direct plugin edits without requiring a privileged job." An operator in that lane can edit the plan file — or `data.json` — directly with any editor, because the phase gates drop there. The injection and the `[JOB-COMPLETE]` counting rule are ceremonies the agent honors at phase entry and at the right cycle, not tamper-proof barriers against an operator who has shell access.]*

## What the plan file teaches

Long-horizon memory is the final mechanism of the markov phasic brain. The discipline and map from [Essay 6.2](06_2-discipline-and-map.html) gave the brain its phase cycle. CONDENSE from [Essay 6.7](06_7-condense.html) gave it metabolism. The multiplier from [Essay 6.8](06_8-inverse-multiplier.html) gave it honest scope. Gmode from [Essay 6.9](06_9-gmode.html) gave it an off-cycle lane. The plan file gives it a *horizon* — the capacity to hold the same plan across days, weeks, dozens of cycles, with the plan re-injecting itself at every phase boundary so the brain never forgets where the work has reached.

That is what makes the markov brain a brain, not a loop. A loop reacts. A brain remembers. The plan file is the substrate of that remembering, and the `.yaml` injection is the reflex that fires it back into the present every time the agent crosses a phase.

The cycle, the organ, the multiplier, gmode, the plan file — each of these is a mechanism. None of them is the *thing they run inside*. Phases are plugins. CONDENSE is a plugin. Gmode rides on a plugin. The plan-file lifecycle is split between `phase_plan` and `job_core`. Everything in this essay series is built on a standardized way of packaging cognitive mechanisms — the plugin kit. That is the subject of the next essay. *[ref: lifecycle-spans-two-plugins | .claude/plugins/ directory layout | The plugin directory shows the packaging: `phase_observe/`, `phase_plan/`, `phase_execute/`, `phase_verify/`, `phase_condense/` are each a plugin; `phasic_system/` is the orchestrator; `job_core/` owns the job lifecycle and the completion-hook pipeline. The plan-file lifecycle lives across two of these — `set-plan-file` and the `.yaml` validators in `phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh`, the job fields and completion hooks in `job_core`. No single plugin owns the lifecycle; the kit is what lets two plugins coordinate one cognitive mechanism.]*

This is the architect's pivot point. Everything from [Essay 5.1's two-layer foundation](../b5/05_1-the-two-layer-foundation.html) through the markov phasic brain we have just finished mapping — was the seed agent's anatomy. You have seen the always-on cortex, the CLAUDE.md hierarchy, the phases, the cognitive metabolism organ, the multiplier dial, gmode's off-cycle lane, the plan file's long-horizon memory. You have seen *how the seed agent thinks*. What you have not yet seen is how a new piece of that cognition gets *built*.

The phases and the waterfall are mechanisms. How do you BUILD a new phase that fits this design? How do you grow a new always-on plugin without breaking the substrate? How do you author your own marker, your own coaching voice, your own subagent definition, and have the rest of the architecture accept them as natively as the prototype's own?

[Essay 7 — The Plugin Kit](../b7/07_1-plugin-kit-foundation.html) is the answer. The brain is built. The kit is what lets you grow new pieces of it safely.
