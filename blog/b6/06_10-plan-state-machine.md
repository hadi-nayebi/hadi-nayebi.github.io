# The Plan File — Long-Horizon Memory

*Essay 6.10 — The Markov Phasic Brain, Part 10 of 10.*

---

[Essay 6.9](06_9-gmode.html) opened the off-cycle lane — the deliberate maintenance side-quest where the phase gates are dropped so the operator can do work that doesn't fit the standard OPEVC shape. Gmode covers a *short* horizon: hours, one sitting, one ceremony. This final essay covers the opposite horizon — work that doesn't fit inside a single cycle and shouldn't. Work that needs to remember itself across days and dozens of cycles.

The mechanism is small. One field on the job object, one document on disk, and a counting rule that decides when the work is done. The discipline it produces is what turns the markov brain from single-session cognition into something that can hold a plan in its head for weeks. *[ref: plan-file-is-long-horizon-substrate | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | The canonical glossary names the plan file (`.md` or `.yaml`) as "the cross-cycle durable handoff vessel. It persists across activations and accumulates intelligence run-over-run." The lifecycle is governed by the job's `cycle` counter and the plan's declared cycle count — not by a separate state machine. The plan file is born in cycle 1 EXECUTE, never at job creation.]*

This essay is for you — especially when your work takes longer than one sitting and the plan needs to outlive any single cycle.

---

## What the plan file owns

The plan file owns the long-horizon memory of a job. One field carries it — `plan_file`, living on the job's `phase_plan` extension (keyed to the job by its ID, stored in `phase_plan`'s own `data.json`): the literal `false` when the job has no plan, or the name of a working document on disk (`<name>.md` or `<name>.yaml`) when it does. The document is the memory; the field is the pointer. *[ref: plan-file-field-three-values | .claude/context/job-system.md "Job Stage" section | The canonical glossary defines `plan_file` as a single value per job — one of `false` (no plan), `<name>.md`, or `<name>.yaml`. A once-only guard prevents flipping the extension on the same job. The job's Stage is read directly from this shape: `false` is Stage 1, `.md` is Stage 2, `.yaml` is Stage 3.]*

The plan file is born in cycle 1 EXECUTE — not at job creation. Every job is created the same minimal way: a name and a short objective. The `plan_file` pointer starts out *undecided* — a set-once sentinel that has not yet been resolved to a value. Nothing on disk yet. The decision of whether this job *needs* a long-horizon document — and what shape it takes — is made later, once the agent has actually looked at the work. *[ref: plan-file-born-cycle-1-execute | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | The glossary is explicit: the on-disk plan file is BORN in cycle 1 EXECUTE, not at job creation. At creation the `plan_file` pointer is the undecided set-once sentinel (null), carried on the job's phase_plan extension — the job record itself has no plan_file key. The cycle-1 PLAN of the activated job owns the decision and calls `set-plan-file` (`false` for Stage 1, `<name>.md` for Stage 2, `<name>.yaml` for Stage 3); the advance out of PLAN is blocked until that call resolves the sentinel. EXECUTE is the phase that creates non-CLAUDE.md files, so it is the phase that writes the Stage 2/3 plan document to disk.]*

What lives where matters. Job-scoped facts live on the job object; file-scoped facts live in the file. Plan files carry a small identification frontmatter — a `job:` line and a `plan_file:` line declaring which job they belong to — plus a `total_cycles:` count (for `.md`) or a `cycles:` list (for `.yaml`) declaring how many cycles the work is planned to take. There is no lifecycle-state field; the job's progress is read directly from its `cycle` counter against that declared total. *[ref: plan-frontmatter-and-cycle-count | .claude/context/job-system.md "Job Stage" section | Stage 2 plans carry "required YAML frontmatter declaring `total_cycles: <N>`"; Stage 3 plans declare a `cycles:` list whose `max(c.cycle for c in cycles)` is the total. The job's completion eligibility is computed from `current_cycle` against that total (see "Cycle counter behavior"), not tracked by a separate state field.]*

## The Stages

A job's *Stage* is its classification by plan-file shape, and it is decided in **cycle 1 PLAN** — not at creation. The cleanest mental model: cycle 1 of any multi-cycle job is for creating or updating its plan; cycle 2 onward is for the actual work. Fresh job? Cycle 1 creates the plan. Re-activated job whose plan already exists? Cycle 1 updates it. Real work always starts cycle 2. *[ref: stage-decided-cycle-1-plan | .claude/context/job-system.md "Job Stage" section | The glossary: Stage is decided in cycle 1 PLAN of the activated job, NOT at creation. The creation objective only IMPLIES a possible Stage; the real decision is made once OBSERVE has gathered the job's context. In cycle 1 PLAN the seed makes the 3-way call via `set-plan-file` — `false` (Stage 1), `<name>.md` (Stage 2), or `<name>.yaml` (Stage 3) — and the PLAN→EXECUTE advance is blocked until that call lands, so the Stage decision cannot be skipped (even Stage 1 requires an explicit `set-plan-file false`). The Stage is then read from `plan_file`'s decided shape; the decision is owned by the activated job's first PLAN phase, not by whatever created it.]*

**Stage 1 — single-cycle, collaborative.** Cycle 1 PLAN makes the explicit Stage-1 call — `set-plan-file false` — resolving the sentinel to `false`. There is no document on disk. The entire OPEVC pass holds the work in working memory and commits the artifacts directly. Single fixes, single updates, a freestyle teaching conversation — anything that closes in one cycle. Backward edges happen *inside* the cycle, between phases, not across cycles. A Stage-1 job can also be the seed from which a repeatable Stage-2 version grows: at CONDENSE, the agent can emit a `[PENDING-JOB]` marker proposing the multi-cycle version of the same work. *[ref: stage-1-no-plan-file | .claude/context/job-system.md "Job Stage" section | Stage 1 is defined as single-cycle, collaborative. Cycle 1 PLAN calls `set-plan-file false`, resolving the undecided sentinel to the decided value `false` — even Stage 1 requires the explicit call, since the PLAN→EXECUTE advance is blocked while the pointer is still undecided. ONE cycle that loops internally via backward transitions; ONE CONDENSE at the end where CONDENSE asks `[JOB-COMPLETE]`. Used for one-off teaching, exploration, simple fixes. At runtime, `plan_file == false` is the decided-state signal the eligibility gate reads as Stage 1.]*

**Stage 2 — repeatable, with an `.md` plan.** Cycle 1 PLAN calls `set-plan-file <name>.md`; cycle 1 EXECUTE writes the document at `.claude/knowledge/plans/<name>.md` with `total_cycles: <N>` in its frontmatter. The document becomes the long-horizon memory of this job: cycles 2..N do operational work against it, VERIFY refines it when a cycle surfaces a better criterion, and every cycle's PLAN re-reads it at phase entry. This is what carries a long job's understanding across days, after chat history has long since rolled off. *[ref: stage-2-md-plan | .claude/context/job-system.md "Job Stage" section | Stage 2: "Cycle 1 PLAN calls `set-plan-file <id> <name>.md`. Cycle 1 EXECUTE creates the plan file at `.claude/knowledge/plans/<name>.md` with required YAML frontmatter declaring `total_cycles: <N>`. … Cycles 2..N do operational work against the plan. … Reactivatable — the plan file persists on disk; subsequent activations get smarter plans."]*

**Stage 3 — repeatable, with a `.yaml` plan.** Cycle 1 PLAN calls `set-plan-file <name>.yaml`; cycle 1 EXECUTE writes a structured document whose `cycles:` list declares each cycle's expected work. In completion semantics it is **identical to Stage 2** — cycle-1 plan creation, cycle-2..N work, the same counting rule deciding when the job is done. The only difference is the file format, and what that format unlocks: the `.yaml` can inject itself into the agent's context at every phase entry. Like any Stage, Stage 3 is a valid starting format — a job can be born Stage 3, decided in cycle-1 PLAN. In practice the `.yaml` is usually authored once a mature Stage-2 `.md` pattern has run enough times to be worth hardening into structure — a fresh job inspired by that pattern, never an automatic flip of the Stage-2 job itself. *[ref: stage-3-identical-except-format | .claude/context/job-system.md "Job Stage" section | Stage 3 is defined as "Otherwise IDENTICAL to Stage 2 in completion semantics … The ONLY difference between Stage 2 and Stage 3 is plan-file format. Inspired typically by a mature Stage-2 job repeated many times — NOT a state-flip on the Stage-2 itself." This corrects the older model that framed the `.yaml` as a separate dependent job spawned from the `.md` job.]*

A future *Stage 4* — a job that ships its own plugin, adding new gates and new voices — is named in the architecture as a future direction, not part of the current prototype. *[ref: stage-4-aspirational | .claude/context/job-system.md "Job Stage" section | "A future Stage 4 is named in B8.2 as the 'plugin form of a job' — a job that ships its own per-job plugin adding new hard gates and new voices. No code referent yet (aspirational per Rule 38's customization-gradient Layer 5)."]*

## How a multi-cycle job knows it is done

There is no state machine walking a plan through named stages. There is a counting rule. *[ref: no-state-machine-only-a-counting-rule | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" + "Cycle counter behavior" sections | The lifecycle has no separate state field and no named approval stages — "Plan file lifecycle" tracks the plan with the `plan_file` value alone (`false`, an `.md`, or a `.yaml`). What governs a multi-cycle job's progress is the cycle counter against the plan's declared total: "Cycle counter behavior" defines `cycle` as monotonic-increment-only, and completion is the counting rule `current_cycle >= effective_last_cycle`, not a walk through named stages.]*

Each multi-cycle plan declares its total — `total_cycles` for an `.md`, the length of the `cycles:` list for a `.yaml`. The job carries a `cycle` counter. The job becomes *eligible* to complete when its current cycle reaches the **effective last cycle**: the declared total, plus any extension cycles the run had to add when the original plan came up short. *[ref: effective-last-cycle-formula | .claude/context/job-system.md "Cycle counter behavior" section | The glossary gives the formula `effective_last_cycle = (plan_file != false ? plan_file.total_cycles : 1) + extension_cycles_added`. The `[JOB-COMPLETE]` eligibility check at CONDENSE fires for Stage 2/3 when `current_cycle >= effective_last_cycle`. The `extension_cycles_added` term lets a run exceed its planned length when work surfaces mid-run (see "Extension cycle").]*

That second term is a small per-run counter on the job — `extension_cycles_added`, starting at zero and climbing by one each time a cycle has to be added. It is what lets the planned total be an estimate the run can honestly exceed rather than a hard ceiling: a `.md` plan that declared four cycles but needed a fifth carries `extension_cycles_added = 1`, and the eligibility check moves with it. It is a *per-run* counter — it resets to zero whenever the job re-activates for a fresh run, so each run forecasts against its own plan rather than inheriting the last run's overruns. *[ref: extension-cycles-added-per-run-counter | .claude/context/job-system.md "`extension_cycles_added` field" + "Cycle counter behavior" sections | The glossary defines `extension_cycles_added` as a per-run integer counter starting at 0, incremented by each `--add-cycle "<text>"` invocation, feeding `effective_last_cycle = (plan_file != false ? plan_file.total_cycles : 1) + extension_cycles_added`. "Cycle counter behavior" lists it among the fields reset to 0 on reactivation (alongside `cycle` and `user_approval`); it does NOT reset on a plain `activate`. The field, the `--add-cycle` command, and the formula are built: `phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh` creates `extension_cycles_added: 0` at job birth, increments it (alongside an `extension_contexts[]` append) on each `--add-cycle`, and resets it to 0 on reactivation; the eligibility gate in `job_core/hooks/question-capture.sh` reads `current_cycle >= effective_last_cycle`.]*

For a Stage-1 job there is no plan file, so `plan_file == false` makes it eligible to complete at *every* CONDENSE — every cycle is potentially the last. For Stage 2 and Stage 3, eligibility waits until the counter reaches the planned end. The value of `plan_file` is doing double duty: it names the document, and its presence-or-absence is what tells the completion gate which rule to apply. *[ref: completion-eligibility-by-plan-file | .claude/context/job-system.md "[JOB-COMPLETE] completion hooks" section | The pre-completion hook's eligibility gate: "`plan_file == false` (Stage 1, fires every CONDENSE) OR `current_cycle >= effective_last_cycle` (Stage 2/3)." A single field, `plan_file`, both points at the document and discriminates Stage 1 from Stage 2/3 at the gate.]*

When the final cycle's CONDENSE arrives and the job is eligible, the agent asks the operator one structured question — `[JOB-COMPLETE]` — presenting the evidence that the objective is met. (That ceremony lands inside CONDENSE, the cognitive organ from [Essay 6.7](06_7-condense.html).) Approval flips the job to `completed`. The `plan_file` is not nulled or archived; it stays exactly where it is. *[ref: completion-plan-file-persists | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | On approval: "`job.sh approve` flips `user_approval = true` → post-completion hook calls `job.sh complete` → status flips to `completed`. `plan_file` STAYS at its value across the completion." The plan is neither nulled nor moved on completion — it persists exactly so it can be re-read on the next activation.]*

That persistence is the whole point of the learning loop below.

VERIFY guards each cycle along the way. If a cycle's `## Outstanding Items` are not empty, VERIFY blocks the forward-advance and the agent loops back to PLAN or EXECUTE to resolve them. And if VERIFY *edits* the plan file — refines a vague criterion, drops one that can't be tested — it cannot forward-advance either; it backward-advances to PLAN, which re-cognizes and routes to OBSERVE for more context, to EXECUTE to redraft the plan, or straight back to VERIFY to re-test the updated plan without re-executing. *[ref: plan-verify-backward-loop | .claude/context/opevc-phases.md "Plan-verify backward loop" section | "Triggered when VERIFY edits the plan file … The custom VERIFY gate detects the plan-file edit and BLOCKS forward-advance. The agent calls `verify-commit.sh --backward plan` … PLAN re-cognizes and forwards to one of three destinations" — backward to OBSERVE, forward to EXECUTE, or forward to VERIFY directly (a planned one-line `plan:verify` FORWARD_MAP entry — an Addition-ledger item not yet in phase.sh). No state-tracking field; the backward loop is built from existing transition primitives; the direct plan→verify forward return is the one piece still pending in code.]*

## How `.yaml` injection turns the brain long-horizon

Both plan files belong to the agent. What changes between them is injection control: the `.md` is loaded by recall or read whole; the `.yaml` is dripped in per phase, granular and consistent.

Once a Stage-3 job is running — its `plan_file` ends in `.yaml` — the phasic system's entry hook reads the `.yaml` on every phase transition and writes the phase-specific field-map to a cache file. The shared voice helper reads that cache when it fires any voice during the new phase, and yaml field-map entries augment the relevant voices — turning a job's structured long-horizon context into a stream of injections that show up automatically wherever the agent is reading guidance. *[ref: phase-init-yaml-injection-on-transition | .claude/plugins/phasic_system/hooks/phase-init.sh write_yaml_cache + phase-transition branch | The write_yaml_cache helper resolves `$ROOT_DIR/.claude/.tmp/yaml-injection-cache.json` and writes the field-map for the focused job's current phase by calling `bash "$PHASE_SH" --hook read-yaml`. The phase-transition branch of phase-init.sh calls write_yaml_cache after every advance, back, or commit-transition event so the freshly entered phase's yaml content immediately augments downstream voices. Gmode enter clears the cache (side-quest isolation); gmode exit restores it. New jobs and focus events also refresh. Net result: phase entry is the injection moment; the .yaml plays back the job's long-horizon memory each time the agent crosses a phase boundary.]*

The augmentation supports append, replace, and prepend modes. A bare-string yaml entry **appends** its text to the rendered voice after a blank line — the back-compat default. A structured entry of the form `{mode: replace, text: "..."}` **replaces** the rendered voice text entirely with the yaml content (useful when the job's context-specific voice should override the universal one). A `{mode: prepend, text: "..."}` entry **prepends** the yaml content before the rendered voice (useful for stamping a job-specific banner above the standard guidance). All three modes apply the same variable substitution as the underlying voice — `{{job_id}}`, `{{focused_name}}`, and the rest substitute identically into the yaml text. *[ref: voice-helper-three-mode-augmentation | .claude/plugins/lib/voice-helper/voice-helper.sh yaml-cognition block | The helper inspects the cache entry's JSON type per voice id. Bare string → APPEND (back-compat); object `{mode, text}` → switch on `mode` between `append` / `replace` / `prepend`; unknown mode falls back to APPEND so unrecognized values cannot silently drop a voice. The same KV_ARGS used to interpolate the xml voice text are re-applied to the yaml text via a preserved-args pattern so substitution keys stay symmetric across the two surfaces.]*

The yaml key IS the voice id directly — no transform, no naming convention. That makes the **callable voice catalog** the contract surface for layer-4 customization: a yaml plan can only target voices that some hook or script actually fires. A new helper builds the catalog by scanning every plugin's `voice.xml` for `id="..."` definitions and every non-test `.sh` / `.py` for quoted references. A defined voice with no callsite is an **orphan** — useless, because it never injects into context, and an invisible trap for yaml authors who target it and watch nothing happen. The seed agent ships with a recurring cleanup job pattern that consumes the orphan list and either links or removes each one over time. *[ref: voice-catalog-and-orphan-discipline | .claude/plugins/lib/yaml-cognition/build-voice-catalog.py + CLAUDE.md | The catalog tool enumerates defined ids across all `voice.xml` files plus quoted references in non-test plugin source (recognizing direct `get_voice "id"`, the `block "id"` wrapper, case-dispatch branches, and array enumerations like `COACHING_IDS=("id" ...)`). Modes: `--audit` (full report), `--list-callable` / `--list-orphans` / `--list-missing` (one id per line). The lib's CLAUDE.md names the contract: a yaml plan's keys must satisfy the callable set; orphans are scheduled for cleanup via a recurring yaml job, not allowed to linger as silent traps.]*

The yaml plan loader enforces the contract at validation time. When PLAN validates a new or edited `.yaml`, the schema check is followed by a key-vs-catalog check: every key under the phases map must match a callable voice id, otherwise the plan is rejected with a `did you mean` suggestion drawn from the closest matches. This catches the most common yaml authoring error — typing a voice id that no longer exists, or never did — before the job ever runs the cycle. *[ref: yaml-key-validator-rejects-uncallable-ids | .claude/plugins/lib/yaml-cognition/validate-yaml-voice-ids.py wired in .claude/plugins/phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh validate-yaml-format | The validator loads the callable set from the catalog builder, walks `cycles[*].phases.<phase>.<key>` (both current and legacy schema shapes), and rejects keys outside the callable set with `difflib.get_close_matches` suggestions. plan.sh's `validate-yaml-format` handler calls the validator AFTER the schema check passes; rejection fires the `error-yaml-voice-ids-invalid` voice with the path + flattened detail. Exit 0 = all keys callable; exit 1 = one or more invalid; exit 2 = catalog build error.]*

A planned job carries its scope-thinking in the plan itself. Phase entry is coached, never gated — there is no ceremony to repeat — and for a Stage-2 or Stage-3 job the coaching arrives pre-loaded: the plan file declares the cycle's work, and the `.yaml` form injects per-phase context straight into the entry voice, so the agent enters each phase already oriented by the document instead of re-deriving the scope from scratch. *[ref: planned-jobs-entry-orientation-from-plan | .claude/context/opevc-rhythm.md "Entry depth forecast" | Scope-thinking at phase entry is COACHED, never required: no entry act, no declaration gate. The seed enters a phase carrying its context — for a planned job, the plan file's declared work — and the entry voice guides it; a depth intuition also informally sizes how much of the phase's work gets delegated.]*

That `3` is a prototype calibration, not a law — a seed that wants its planned jobs to still run deep could lower the default or derive it from the plan's declared scope; what the architecture fixes is that the forecast already lives in the plan, not which number stands in for it.

This is the long-horizon mechanism. Without it, every cycle would start cold — the agent would have to re-read the plan and re-derive what each phase should know. With it, the agent's context grows back the long-form structure of the job automatically, each cycle, at every phase boundary. The brain doesn't have to remember to remember. *[ref: phase-entry-is-injection-moment | .claude/plugins/phasic_system/hooks/phase-init.sh write_yaml_cache + the phase-transition / phase-commit-transition branch | The phase-transition branch calls `write_yaml_cache` after every advance, back, and commit-transition event. The write resolves the focused job's current phase and calls `bash "$PHASE_SH" --hook read-yaml` to extract the field-map for that phase, writing it to `$ROOT_DIR/.claude/.tmp/yaml-injection-cache.json`. Subsequent voices read the cache automatically. Without this hook, the cache wouldn't refresh on phase boundaries and the `.yaml` would be dead documentation; the hook IS the injection discipline.]*

The `.md` and `.yaml` are not redundant — they are different surfaces of the same agent-facing plan. The `.md` is a flexible prose plan the agent works from by recall, suited to a job still maturing. The `.yaml` is the more controlled form: a structured plan the agent injects into context per phase, consistently, at every boundary. They share a basename so the agent knows they belong together, but they differ in injection granularity. *[ref: md-yaml-coexist-different-surfaces | .claude/plugins/phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh validate-yaml-format | The validate-yaml-format command checks the `.yaml` against the phase.sh read-yaml schema — phases as keys, each carrying structured fields the per-phase injection consumes. The `.yaml`'s structure is what makes granular per-phase injection possible; the `.md` carries the same plan in a looser form the agent loads by recall. Both are the agent's plan and both can be studied by the operator.]*

## The learning loop

A plan file that persists across completions is a plan file that can learn.

When a repeatable job finishes, it does not vanish. Its plan file stays on disk, and the job can be re-activated later — manually, or automatically on a repeating interval when the seed agent compacts and scans for due jobs. On re-activation the cycle counter resets to zero and the run starts fresh, but the plan file survives, and so does one quieter record: the job's `extension_contexts` — the running log of every time a past run had to add a cycle because the declared plan came up short. *[ref: extension-contexts-history-mode | .claude/context/job-system.md "extension_contexts field" section | "Opt-in history mode: append-only across runs (NOT reset on reactivation). The array grows monotonically over the job's lifetime, accumulating one entry per `--add-cycle '<text>'` invocation across all activations." The cycle counter, `user_approval`, and `extension_cycles_added` all reset on reactivation; `extension_contexts[]`, `plan_file`, and `plugin_lock_approval` persist.]*

That automatic re-activation is built, and it is worth being precise about how it works — because the answer is deliberately small. While the seed agent is working, there is no separate scheduler. It already compacts its own context on a rhythm; the design couples a scan to that same moment. Every time the agent self-compacts, it walks its completed jobs and, for each one whose repeating interval has elapsed since it last finished, flips exactly that job back to life — one atomic re-activation per due job, no batch command. The flip resets what a fresh run needs reset (the cycle counter, the approval flag, the per-run extension counter) and preserves what carries the job's identity forward (the plan file, the extension history, any plugin-edit approval the job earned). While the seed agent is working, its own rhythm *is* the schedule. *[ref: self-compact-reactivation-rhythm-is-the-schedule | .claude/plugins/brain_guard/scripts/self-compact.sh STEP 4.8 + .claude/plugins/job_core/scripts/scan-repeating-due.sh | Heartbeat-1 of the two-heartbeat reactivation model — the WORKING-phase driver, built at STEP 4.8 of self-compact. The no-scheduler property is scoped to the working phase: while the seed is working (jobs `active`/`pending`, the Stop gate blocking), self-compact fires often enough that its own rhythm IS the schedule — no cron, timer, or daemon needed *here*; it does NOT extend to the quiescent phase. STEP 4.8 invokes `scan-repeating-due.sh`, which selects completed jobs whose `repeating_interval` has elapsed since `last_completed_at` and loops one `job.sh --hook reactivate` per match (single-mutation contract). Each reactivated `pending` job is picked up by the seed's normal active-first / activate-pending next-job-selection. The quiescent phase has its own timer-daemon (Heartbeat-2, below).]*

There is one deliberate exception, and it lives at the other edge of the seed agent's life. When no active or pending work remains and the seed goes fully idle, the working rhythm is gone — there is nothing left to ride a scan on. So the stop boundary launches a separate mechanism to carry reactivation while the seed is stopped: a small, single-instance timer-daemon that watches the same completed jobs and wakes the seed when one comes due. It is the one place the architecture admits a timer, by design — a stopped seed has no rhythm of its own, so the quiescent phase gets one.

When the daemon finds due jobs, it reactivates every one of them to pending, equally — no priority, no urgency ranking. Then it does exactly as much as the situation requires, and no more. If a single job came due, there is no decision to make about what to work on next, so the daemon activates and focuses that job itself and wakes the seed straight into it. If several came due, the choice of what to work first belongs to the seed, not the daemon — so the daemon leaves them all pending and just wakes the seed to survey and choose.

Either way the wake is a prompt carrying a reserved `[WAKE]` marker, and that marker is load-bearing: a wake arrives at an idle seed with no active job, which is exactly the condition that would otherwise make the agent bootstrap a brand-new job out of the prompt. The marker tells that bootstrap to stand down — this is a wake into existing work, not a new request. *[ref: quiescent-phase-heartbeat-timer-daemon | .claude/plugins/job_core/scripts/quiescent-heartbeat.sh do_tick + .claude/plugins/job_core/hooks/prompt-handler.sh [WAKE] branch | Heartbeat-2 of the two-heartbeat model — the detached, single-instance daemon launched by the Stop gate's SUCCESS branch (`stop-gate.sh`, where active+pending == 0). Each tick runs the same `scan-repeating-due.sh` as Heartbeat-1, reactivating EVERY due job to `pending` equally. Then `do_tick` branches on the count: exactly one due → the daemon also `activate`s + `focus`es that job and injects the `wake-single` prompt; more than one → it leaves them all `pending` and injects the `wake-multi` prompt (the seed picks focus). Both prompts carry the literal `[WAKE]` marker; `prompt-handler.sh` detects it and SKIPS its no-active-job bootstrap — routing to next-job-selection instead of creating a phantom job. The daemon self-terminates once the seed is awake, on pane death, or at TTL.]*

Cycle 1 of the new run reads that record. The OBSERVE entry voice surfaces it directly: prior runs added these late extensions; consider whether your refined plan should absorb them upfront. PLAN and EXECUTE then update the plan file — adjust `total_cycles`, rewrite a cycle's entry — so the next run plans for what the last run only discovered halfway through. *[ref: cycle-1-observe-reads-extension-history | .claude/context/job-system.md "Extension cycle" section | On reactivation, "Cycle 1 of the repeat run reads the history. OBSERVE entry voice fires: 'Prior runs added these late extensions … Consider whether your refined PLAN this cycle should ABSORB these into the plan file (.md `total_cycles` adjustment + per-cycle entries, OR .yaml `cycles[]` entries) so future runs don't need to re-add them.'" The extensions are the learning trail across reactivations.]*

Over many runs the job converges toward needing no extensions at all. The plan stops being a guess and becomes an accurate forecast of the work, because each run wrote its surprises back into it. The learning is structural, not memorized — a future run reads the better plan directly, the same way it would read any other plan. This is what makes a repeating job get *smarter*, not just *repeat*. *[ref: learning-over-time-structural | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | "The cycle-level VERIFY gate + job-level CONDENSE gate + cross-run `extension_contexts[]` history form a closed feedback loop. The plan file (`.md` or `.yaml`) absorbs experience run-over-run; the job converges toward 'no extensions needed' as the plan increasingly captures real scope. … The learning is structural, not memorized — future runs read the refined plan file directly."]*

## Where the plan file lives

So far this essay has placed the plan file at `.claude/knowledge/plans/` — where the prototype writes it today. A newer layer of the design gives it a more deliberate home: the job's own **run-aware directory**, `.claude/jobs/<job-id>/` — *run-aware* because each activation of the job, reactivations included, gets its own numbered `run-N/` space — where everything a job accumulates across its runs lives together, each run a recorded practice the future seed can mine. *[ref: run-aware-job-directory | .claude/context/job-system.md "Run-aware job directory" section | The consolidated term defines `.claude/jobs/<job_id>/` as the per-job directory that homes everything a job accumulates across its runs: a job-level `plan.{md,yaml}` plus `run-<r>/` subdirectories holding `compaction-<seq>.md` (one per session in the run) and `session-log-<c>.md` (CONDENSE output, one or more per run). It folds in two relocations — the plan file moves from `.claude/knowledge/plans/` to job level, and the CONDENSE step-7 session archive moves from `.claude/knowledge/session/` to `run-<r>/session-log-<c>.md`. pending: code not yet aligned — the prototype today writes plan files to `knowledge/plans/` and session archives to `knowledge/session/`.]*

The layout puts the plan at the **job level** and everything per-run beneath it:

```
.claude/jobs/<job-id>/
  plan.md | plan.yaml        # job-level: persists and refines across runs
  run-1/
    compaction-1.md          # one per session in the run
    session-log-1.md         # CONDENSE output; one or more per run
  run-2/
    ...
```

The plan file sits at the job level, not inside any run — so a reactivation reads the same refined plan the last run left behind, which is the learning loop above. What lives *inside* each `run-<r>/` is that run's record: the **compaction files** the agent seals at each session boundary, and the **session logs** CONDENSE writes at the end of a cycle. Over many runs the seed can read across `run-*/` to learn how it does this kind of job — which is the point of recording each run as practice.

The plan file shares this directory with a sibling document that points the opposite way. The plan file is **forward and operational** — it carries cross-*cycle* work state: what each cycle does, its acceptance criteria, the structure the next cycle inherits.

The **compaction file** from [the always-on cortex](../b5/05_3-brain-guard.html) is **backward and reflective** — it carries cross-*session* cognitive state: how the work went, the loops the agent got stuck in, the blind spots it hit, and the handful of top-level facts that must survive the next context clear. *[ref: compaction-vs-plan-boundary | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" section (Boundary vs the compaction file) + .claude/context/brain-memory.md "Compaction file" section | The Plan-file-lifecycle boundary paragraph states the plan file carries cross-cycle WORK state and is forward/operational, while the compaction file carries cross-session COGNITIVE state and is backward/reflective; both now live in the run-aware job directory — the plan file at job level (relocated from `.claude/knowledge/plans/`), the compaction files under `run-<r>/`. The Compaction file term (.claude/context/brain-memory.md) defines it as the per-job cognition-about-the-job, built across phase exits and carried across sessions, never condensed. pending: code not yet aligned.]*

The two never blur. Ask of any note: is this what the next *cycle* needs to do, or what the next *session* needs to remember? Work state goes in the plan; cognition about the work goes in the compaction file. They are siblings in the same job directory precisely because keeping them apart — one forward, one backward — is what lets a long job both *run* coherently across cycles and *think about itself* across sessions.

This run-aware home and its compaction-file sibling are where the design is heading; the prototype today still writes the plan file to `knowledge/plans/` and its session archives to `knowledge/session/`.

## What would break without it

Take the plan file out. Now every multi-cycle job has to hold its own plan in working memory or re-derive it each session. The agent can still attempt long-horizon work, but each cycle starts cold — re-reading whatever notes survive in `knowledge/`, rebuilding intuition the previous cycle already had. Long jobs drift. A late cycle of a migration contradicts an early one because it never read the earlier plan. *[ref: plan-file-is-cross-cycle-memory | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | The plan file exists precisely as "the cross-cycle durable handoff vessel" so phases can coordinate across cycles without rebuilding the contract every time. Remove it and each cycle reconstructs the plan from heuristics — the failure mode of chat-history-based agents on multi-session work.]*

Take the `.yaml` injection out. Now the long-horizon context is a document the agent has to *remember* to re-read every cycle. Sometimes it does. Sometimes it doesn't. Injection at phase entry is the discipline that turns "should re-read the plan" into "the plan re-reads itself into the context, every phase, every cycle." *[ref: injection-is-the-discipline | .claude/plugins/phasic_system/hooks/phase-init.sh write_yaml_cache helper | The write_yaml_cache helper reads `${YAML_INJECTION_CACHE:-$ROOT_DIR/.claude/.tmp/yaml-injection-cache.json}` and writes the focused job's current-phase field-map. The cache only exists because the `.yaml` exists; without a parseable companion the voice helper has nothing to append and the agent gets generic voices instead of context-loaded ones. The hook turns "should re-read the plan" into automatic re-injection at every phase boundary.]*

Take the persistence out — null the plan file when the job completes. Now the learning loop dies. Every re-activation starts from a blank plan; the run repeats the same mistakes the last run already paid for. The plan can never become a forecast because nothing carries forward to forecast from. *[ref: persistence-enables-learning | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | Because `plan_file` and `extension_contexts[]` both persist across completion ("`plan_file` STAYS at its value across the completion"; `extension_contexts[]` "STAYS in history mode"), the next activation can read the refined plan and the prior runs' extensions. Nulling the plan on completion would sever this loop — which is exactly why the plan file persists instead.]*

Each layer closes one failure mode of long-horizon work that ordinary chat-history-based agents fail at.

## What you would customize

The plan file is the clearest example of the seed's fourth customization layer — the place where a single job tailors the agent's behavior without touching a line of code. *[ref: plan-file-is-layer-4 | .claude/plugins/phasic_system/hooks/phase-init.sh write_yaml_cache | The `.yaml` plan customizes soft controls per job without touching code: write_yaml_cache reads its per-cycle, per-phase keys at every phase entry and voice-helper.sh augments the matching voices at fire time. This per-job tailoring of soft controls is the customization gradient's fourth layer — distinct from editing hard gates (code) or the voice catalog (xml). The plan file is data the job carries, not machinery it changes.]*

You would design the **`.yaml` schema**. What does each phase need at entry? OBSERVE may want a `recall_targets:` list; PLAN a `decisions_locked:` map; EXECUTE a `forbidden_files:` array; VERIFY an `acceptance_criteria:` list with pass/fail rows. The prototype ships a minimal injection — roughly one objective per phase. Your seed will discover what richer fields produce richer cognition for your work, and the `.yaml` is where that discovery lives. *[ref: yaml-schema-is-customizable | .claude/plugins/phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh validate-yaml-format | The validate-yaml-format handler checks the `.yaml` against the phase.sh read-yaml contract — phases as keys, structured fields under each. The schema is enforced by the validator, but the validator is yours to edit: add expected fields, change rules, define new injection paths. The minimal version the prototype ships is a starting point, not a ceiling; the brain's long-horizon cognition is exactly as rich as the schema you author.]*

You would tune the **Stage decision**. The current rule lives in cycle-1 PLAN's judgment: a big project likely to span days and revisions gets a plan file; a one-off does not. If your domain has a clear threshold — every matter past a certain size goes multi-cycle — you can codify that as a hint voice that steers the classification. *[ref: stage-decision-is-judgment-shaped | .claude/context/job-system.md "Job Stage" section | The Stage decision is owned by cycle 1 PLAN as narrative judgment, not a hard gate. Codifying a threshold means adding a voice or script that fires on a measurable trigger (size, prior-job similarity, domain tag) and steers the classification. The prototype keeps it judgment-shaped because the trade-offs differ per seed.]*

You would set the **repeating interval**. A job that should re-run on a rhythm — a weekly review, a daily digest — carries an interval; when it elapses, the seed agent's self-compact scan re-activates the job automatically — or, once the seed has gone idle, the quiescent-phase daemon does — and the learning loop above kicks in run-over-run. *[ref: repeating-interval-customization | .claude/context/job-system.md "Repeating job" section | A repeating job sets `repeating_interval` (integer hours, default 0 = one-shot); completed instances auto-reactivate via the self-compact rhythm when `now - last_completed_at >= repeating_interval`. Promotion happens via the CONDENSE-only `[REPEAT-JOB]` ceremony. This is the mechanism that turns a Stage-2/3 job into a self-maintaining recurring one.]*

The interval is a single field — `repeating_interval`, measured in whole hours, defaulting to zero for the ordinary one-shot job. Hours is the unit on purpose: not seconds, not a cron expression, just a plain integer count of hours. You would not type the raw number, though. The promotion question offers a base unit — Hourly, Daily, or Weekly — and a value, and resolves the two into hours behind the scenes (a multiplier of one, twenty-four, or a hundred sixty-eight against your typed value). "Daily" with a value of two is forty-eight hours; "Weekly" alone is a hundred sixty-eight. The scan then reads that interval against one companion field — `last_completed_at`, the moment the job last finished — and fires when enough hours have passed. *[ref: repeating-interval-field-hours-and-base-units | .claude/plugins/job_core/scripts/job.sh job schema + .claude/context/job-system.md "`repeating_interval` field" / "`last_completed_at` field" | `repeating_interval` is an integer-HOURS field, default 0 (one-shot) — hours is the canonical on-disk unit. The `[REPEAT-JOB]` body presents base-unit options mapping to hour multipliers (Hourly ×1 / Daily ×24 / Weekly ×168) plus a typed value; the post-handler computes `repeating_interval = multiplier × value`. The scan reads it alongside `last_completed_at` to compute `(last_completed_at + repeating_interval*3600) < now()`. Both fields are built into the job schema in `job.sh` (every job is born with `repeating_interval: 0, last_completed_at: 0`); `last_completed_at` is written at the `status → completed` flip.]*

`last_completed_at` earns its own dedicated field rather than reusing the job's general "last updated" timestamp, and the reason is precise: the next fire is anchored to the *completion event*, not to any incidental edit afterward. A dedicated field, written exactly once at the moment a job flips to completed, means a later metadata touch — healing a stale value, correcting a label — cannot quietly shift the next fire forward. A job that has never completed carries zero there, and the scan skips it: no prior fire, nothing to measure an interval from. *[ref: last-completed-at-dedicated-anchor | .claude/plugins/job_core/scripts/job.sh complete path + .claude/context/job-system.md "`last_completed_at` field" | Integer-seconds (Unix epoch) field, default 0 (never completed), written exactly once per `status → completed` flip — `job.sh`'s complete path sets `.status="completed" | .last_completed_at=$_lca`. Why a dedicated field? It is the anchor for `repeating_interval` re-fire computation; without it, any post-completion `update_job` call would advance `updated_at` and shift the next fire forward unpredictably. `scan-repeating-due.sh` skips jobs with `last_completed_at == 0` (no prior fire to compare against). Built — a supporting field for the repeating mechanism.]*

You would decide how a job becomes repeating in the first place — through the **`[REPEAT-JOB]` promotion**. It is a prefixed question the agent raises at the end of a cycle, mirroring the same ceremony that already gates the creation of a plugin-touching job: a phase-restricted, shape-checked question on one side and a handler that writes the interval on the other. It fires only during CONDENSE, the phase where the agent already reflects on what comes next, and a coaching voice at that phase entry nudges the agent to consider it — *if this job's work would recur on a cadence, promote it; otherwise leave it one-shot.* The promotion captures two things: the interval (base unit and value) and whether the re-fire should land the job straight into active work or queue it behind whatever is already running — an urgent hourly health-check wants the former, a quiet weekly review the latter. One-shot stays the default; the promotion is the deliberate exception. *[ref: repeat-job-promotion-ceremony | .claude/plugins/job_core/hooks/question-capture.sh [REPEAT-JOB] gate + .claude/context/job-system.md "`[REPEAT-JOB]` prefixed question" | The AskUserQuestion prefix that promotes the focused job from one-shot to Repeating. Mirrors the `[JOB-APPROVE-CREATION]` ceremony — Pre-side phase gate + shape gate + Post-side handler + per-prefix voice quartet. Phase restriction is enforced built: `question-capture.sh` BLOCKS a `[REPEAT-JOB]` question unless `current_phase == condense` and the focused job is multi-cycle and at its final cycle. A phase-entry coaching voice (`consider-repeat-job`) nudges the agent: one-shot is the default; only promote when the cadence is real. The body captures interval (base-unit choice + typed value) AND a reactivate-target (pending default / active for urgent cadence). The question prefix is a registry term cross-referenced from the question-discipline catalog.]*

What you would NOT do is remove the plan file. Take it out and the brain reverts to single-session cognition. The whole markov phasic discipline — the cycle, the cognitive metabolism organ, the rhythm, gmode — is built on the assumption that some jobs deserve to be remembered across cycles. The plan file is what makes that remembering deterministic instead of accidental.

Lift the pattern out of the seed agent and into a law practice. A partner running a long matter carries the plan file the same way: the prose plan accumulates across weekly client-review cycles in `matter.md`; once the matter is large enough to span many sessions, it graduates to a `matter.yaml` whose structured fields — scope, fee structure, retention terms, conflict-check results — inject at every revision phase, so each follow-up cycle inherits the matter's structure automatically instead of re-reading the prose. Run the same kind of matter again next quarter, and last quarter's surprises are already written into the plan. *[ref: pattern-lifts-to-professional-long-horizon | .claude/context/job-system.md "Plan file lifecycle" section | The section establishes the lifecycle as constant in its mechanics — born in cycle 1 EXECUTE, persisting across activations, learning run-over-run: "the plan file … absorbs experience run-over-run; the job converges toward 'no extensions needed' … The learning is structural, not memorized — future runs read the refined plan file directly." It is these domain-independent mechanics that the professional analogy in this paragraph lifts; the per-domain variation (the Stage decision, the `.yaml` schema, the repeating interval — each cited in "What you would customize" above) is the customization surface, not the lifecycle itself.]*

The plan file is friction, not a wall. An operator in [gmode](06_9-gmode.html) can edit it directly with any editor. The injection assumes the phasic system fires on every phase entry; the counting rule assumes the agent honors the `[JOB-COMPLETE]` ceremony at the right cycle. The architecture is the ceremony; the discipline rests on the agent honoring it, not on the gate being unbreakable. *[ref: ceremony-not-tamperproof-barrier | .claude/context/opevc-phases.md "Phase" section | The glossary defines gmode as the "operator's privileged maintenance lane … unlocks `[PLUGIN-LOCK]` for direct plugin edits without requiring a privileged job." An operator in that lane can edit the plan file — or `data.json` — directly with any editor, because the phase gates drop there. The injection and the `[JOB-COMPLETE]` counting rule are ceremonies the agent honors at phase entry and at the right cycle, not tamper-proof barriers against an operator who has shell access.]*

## What the plan file teaches

Long-horizon memory is the final mechanism of the markov phasic brain. The discipline and map from [Essay 6.2](06_2-discipline-and-map.html) gave the brain its phase cycle. CONDENSE from [Essay 6.7](06_7-condense.html) gave it metabolism. The rhythm from [Essay 6.8](06_8-inverse-multiplier.html) gave it honest pacing. Gmode from [Essay 6.9](06_9-gmode.html) gave it an off-cycle lane. The plan file gives it a *horizon* — the capacity to hold the same plan across days, weeks, dozens of cycles, with the plan re-injecting itself at every phase boundary so the brain never forgets where the work has reached.

That is what makes the markov brain a brain, not a loop. A loop reacts. A brain remembers. The plan file is the substrate of that remembering, and the `.yaml` injection is the reflex that fires it back into the present every time the agent crosses a phase.

The cycle, the organ, the rhythm, gmode, the plan file — each of these is a mechanism. None of them is the *thing they run inside*. Phases are plugins. CONDENSE is a plugin. Gmode rides on a plugin. The plan-file lifecycle is split between `phase_plan` and `job_core`. Everything in this essay series is built on a standardized way of packaging cognitive mechanisms — the plugin kit. That is the subject of the next essay. *[ref: lifecycle-spans-two-plugins | .claude/plugins/ directory layout | The plugin directory shows the packaging: `phase_observe/`, `phase_plan/`, `phase_execute/`, `phase_verify/`, `phase_condense/` are each a plugin; `phasic_system/` is the orchestrator; `job_core/` owns the job lifecycle and the completion-hook pipeline. The plan-file lifecycle lives across two of these — `set-plan-file` and the `.yaml` validators in `phase_plan/scripts/plan.sh`, the job fields and completion hooks in `job_core`. No single plugin owns the lifecycle; the kit is what lets two plugins coordinate one cognitive mechanism.]*

This is the architect's pivot point. Everything from [Essay 5.1's two-layer foundation](../b5/05_1-the-two-layer-foundation.html) through the markov phasic brain we have just finished mapping — was the seed agent's anatomy. You have seen the always-on cortex, the CLAUDE.md hierarchy, the phases, the cognitive metabolism organ, the rhythm of work, gmode's off-cycle lane, the plan file's long-horizon memory. You have seen *how the seed agent thinks*. What you have not yet seen is how a new piece of that cognition gets *built*.

The phases and the waterfall are mechanisms. How do you BUILD a new phase that fits this design? How do you grow a new always-on plugin without breaking the substrate? How do you author your own marker, your own coaching voice, your own subagent definition, and have the rest of the architecture accept them as natively as the prototype's own?

[Essay 7 — The Plugin Kit](../b7/07_1-plugin-kit-foundation.html) is the answer. The brain is built. The kit is what lets you grow new pieces of it safely.
